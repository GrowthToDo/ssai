## TL;DR

TCP Scheduling Software (formerly Aladtec) is a self-serve platform: your nurse manager configures it, builds schedules in it, and manages exceptions through it. A managed scheduling service runs the scheduling function for her: a specialist generates draft schedules, she reviews and approves. For a 25-bed critical access hospital with one nurse manager and no scheduling coordinator, the operating model determines whether scheduling time drops from 8 to 12 hours per week to 4 to 6 hours (TCP) or 1 to 2 hours (managed service).

## What TCP Scheduling Software Actually Is

TCP Scheduling Software, the product formerly known as Aladtec, is a self-serve workforce management platform used by healthcare organizations, EMS agencies, and fire departments. It provides scheduling tools, availability management, overtime tracking, and basic compliance features.

"Self-serve" is the key phrase. TCP provides the structure and the tools. Your team provides the labor to operate it.

In practice at a critical access hospital, this means the nurse manager:

- Configures the initial system (entering all nurses, certifications, unit rules, scheduling constraints)
- Builds the schedule each cycle using TCP's interface
- Manages availability changes as they come in
- Handles callout coverage by checking the platform and making replacement calls
- Maintains the certification records and unit assignments as they change
- Learns and stays current on the platform's features

This is a meaningful workload on top of clinical supervision responsibilities. TCP automates parts of the process, but the nurse manager is the operator.

## What a Managed Scheduling Service Does Differently

A managed scheduling service changes the operating model. Instead of software your nurse manager runs, it is a service a specialist runs on her behalf.

With SimpleScheduleAI, the managed service for critical access hospitals:

- A scheduling specialist handles initial setup from your roster upload (Excel, 3 to 5 days)
- The specialist builds draft schedule options each cycle, checking overtime thresholds and certification constraints before presenting drafts
- The nurse manager reviews draft schedules and approves or requests adjustments
- Callout coverage shortlists come pre-ranked by certification, overtime status, and availability
- Every change is automatically logged for [CMS §485.635](https://www.cms.gov/medicare/health-safety-standards/certification-compliance/critical-access-hospitals) audit trail
- The specialist maintains the roster as staff changes occur

The nurse manager's role shifts from operator to approver. She retains final authority over every scheduling decision. The upstream work, the building, checking, and tracking, is handled for her.

## The Honest Trade-Off

TCP has one significant advantage over SimpleScheduleAI: a staff-facing mobile app. Nurses can view their schedules, request availability changes, and participate in shift swaps through the TCP app. SimpleScheduleAI does not have a nurse-facing interface. Nurses receive schedules through whatever communication method the facility uses (email, group text, printed copies).

If nurse self-service scheduling, nurses managing their own availability and viewing schedules on their phones, is a priority for your facility, TCP or another platform with a nurse app is the right choice.

If the priority is reducing the nurse manager's administrative burden and getting operational quickly, the managed service model addresses that more directly.

## Time to Value

One practical comparison that matters when scheduling burden is acute:

**TCP:** 2 to 4 weeks from contract to first operational schedule. This includes configuration time (entering all nurses, certifications, unit rules), learning period, and calibration cycles. A nurse manager in this phase is still running scheduling manually while also learning a new platform.

**Managed service:** 3 to 5 business days from contract to first draft schedule. The specialist handles configuration. The first scheduling cycle begins almost immediately.

For a nurse manager who is burned out on scheduling, experiencing weekly callout scrambles, and considering leaving, four weeks of additional manual scheduling during an implementation is a meaningful cost.

## Which Fits Your CAH?

**Choose TCP Scheduling Software if:**

- Your nurse manager wants direct hands-on control over every scheduling decision
- She is technically comfortable and willing to invest time learning and maintaining the platform
- Staff self-service features (nurses viewing schedules, requesting swaps via mobile app) are important
- 4 to 6 hours of weekly scheduling work is acceptable
- Your facility has minimal IT support available

**Choose a managed scheduling service if:**

- Structural burden reduction is the goal, not just better tools
- Your nurse manager is already stretched thin and cannot absorb a platform learning curve
- Callout coverage scrambles are a specific, recurring pain point
- You need to be operational in days, not weeks
- Delegating schedule draft generation feels appropriate while retaining approval authority

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is TCP Scheduling Software the same as Aladtec?**

Yes. TCP acquired Aladtec in 2021. The platform continues to operate under the Aladtec interface for most existing customers, but new sales and documentation use the TCP Scheduling Software branding. The product is the same.

**Q: What does TCP Scheduling Software cost for a small hospital?**

TCP pricing is not publicly listed and varies by organization size and contract terms. Mid-market scheduling platforms in this category typically run $200 to $600 per month for facilities under 50 beds. Enterprise contracts can be significantly higher.

**Q: How long does it take to implement TCP Scheduling Software at a CAH?**

Implementation typically runs 2 to 4 weeks from contract to first operational schedule, assuming the nurse manager can dedicate 5 to 10 hours to configuration. Facilities with complex staffing rules or large rosters may take longer.

**Q: What happens when someone calls out at 5am on a TCP implementation vs. a managed service?**

With TCP: the nurse manager opens the platform, checks availability, reviews overtime status manually, and makes replacement calls. The platform provides information; she does the work.

With a managed service: the specialist has already maintained a ranked callout shortlist. The nurse manager contacts the first available nurse on the list. Average time: 10 to 15 minutes instead of 30 to 60 minutes of manual research.

## Key Takeaways

- TCP Scheduling Software is a self-serve platform; a managed service is a function someone runs for you. The operating model difference determines how much burden actually decreases
- TCP reduces scheduling from 8 to 12 hours per week to 4 to 6 hours; a managed service reduces it to 1 to 2 hours
- TCP has a staff-facing mobile app (SimpleScheduleAI does not); this is the main reason to choose TCP over a managed service
- Managed services onboard in days vs. weeks for self-serve platforms
- The right choice depends on whether your nurse manager wants to operate a scheduling platform or have the scheduling work done for her
