### SimpleScheduleAI

SimpleScheduleAI is the right fit for critical access hospitals where the nurse manager is spending 8-12 hours per week on scheduling and wants to hand that work to a team that handles it. The managed service model means a specialist does the weekly schedule build, manages callout coverage logic, and tracks compliance, the nurse manager approves the final schedule rather than building it from scratch.

That model works well for CAHs with stable but small staff pools, hospitals where the nurse manager is also clinically active and should not be spending weekend evenings rebuilding a schedule after a callout, and hospitals that have tried self-serve software and found the configuration and maintenance burden was not worth it.

SimpleScheduleAI is not the right fit if you want to manage the scheduling process entirely in-house with your own software configuration.

### Aladtec (TCP Scheduling)

Aladtec is the strongest pure-software option for CAHs that want to manage scheduling in-house with a capable platform. It is healthcare-specific, has reasonable implementation timelines (2-4 weeks), and covers the core CAH compliance requirements. If you want self-serve software with solid CAH features, Aladtec is the primary alternative to consider.

Known limitations: post-acquisition support quality has declined per G2 reviewers, the mobile experience is functional but not modern, and the nurse manager still carries the full operational burden.

### NurseGrid

NurseGrid works as a nurse communication tool. It does not work as a primary scheduling system for a CAH, no FLSA overtime tracking, no CMS audit trail, and the manager-facing app is non-functional per multiple reviewers. If nurse self-service mobile features matter, NurseGrid can be a secondary communication layer alongside a more capable scheduling system.

### SmartLinx

SmartLinx is a healthcare workforce management platform primarily serving long-term care and post-acute organizations. It has compliance features and integrations that go beyond Aladtec, but requires IT involvement and is better suited to 50-200 bed facilities than a 25-bed CAH.

### Deputy

Deputy is a general scheduling platform with a healthcare vertical. It is easy to implement and affordable. It does not cover healthcare credential requirements, CMS documentation, or clinical callout coverage logic. For a CAH with CMS Conditions of Participation requirements, Deputy's compliance gaps are disqualifying.

## What Is the Total Cost of Nurse Scheduling Software for a 25-Bed Hospital?

The total cost calculation includes more than the subscription fee:

**Subscription or service cost.** Self-serve SaaS platforms typically run $200-$600 per month for a 15-25 nurse team. Managed services are quote-based. Both categories are substantially less than the estimated $26,000 per year in manager time that manual scheduling consumes at $50/hour loaded rate.

**Configuration cost.** Self-serve software requires someone to configure scheduling rules, enter staff profiles, and set up compliance requirements. For a nurse manager doing this without IT support, budget 20-40 hours of setup time. A managed service handles this as part of onboarding.

**Training cost.** Any new system requires staff orientation. Simple scheduling apps may need 1-2 hours per person; more complex platforms may require formal training sessions.

**Opportunity cost of switching.** Budget for 2-4 weeks of parallel running while staff adapts. This is a one-time cost, not ongoing.

Most CAHs see positive ROI from scheduling tools within the first quarter when manager time savings and overtime reduction are counted together.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need a contract to use nurse scheduling software, or can I go month-to-month?**

Most SaaS scheduling platforms offer month-to-month pricing at a premium and annual contracts at a discount (typically 10-20% off). For a pilot or evaluation period, month-to-month is the lower-risk starting point. Managed services typically require a minimum engagement to justify setup costs.

**Can nurse scheduling software integrate with our EHR?**

Integration capability varies significantly by platform. Some scheduling tools offer direct integration with Epic, Cerner, or Meditech; others export data in formats compatible with HR and payroll systems. Verify specific integration support with each vendor before committing. For most CAHs, standalone scheduling without EHR integration is the more practical choice, it avoids IT complexity while still covering scheduling and compliance requirements.

**What happens to our scheduling data if we switch platforms?**

Most platforms export scheduling history as CSV or PDF. Before switching, download all historical data and confirm your new platform can import or store it. For compliance purposes, retain at least two years of scheduling documentation regardless of which software you use.

**What are the five features that separate healthcare scheduling software from generic tools?**

Proactive FLSA overtime tracking (flags during schedule building, not after payroll), certification-unit matching enforcement, CMS-compliant change audit trail, clinical callout coverage logic (ranked certified replacement list), and per-diem pool management. These features make scheduling software healthcare-specific rather than industry-generic.

**Is a managed service more expensive than scheduling software for a small hospital?**

Both are typically in the same pricing range for a 25-bed facility. The relevant comparison is total cost: platform cost plus the nurse manager's time operating it. A managed service bundles both; a platform separates them. Recovering 3-4 hours per week of nurse manager time over a year represents meaningful operational value beyond the direct cost comparison.
