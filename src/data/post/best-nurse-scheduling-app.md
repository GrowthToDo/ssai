## Key Takeaways

- Most apps marketed as "nurse scheduling apps" are general-purpose workforce scheduling tools with healthcare branding. The underlying scheduling engine does not know the difference between a nurse and a barista.
- The critical features for hospital nursing, credential enforcement, FLSA 8-and-80 overtime logic, charge nurse coverage, and CMS documentation, are absent from most apps and require manual configuration in others.
- SimpleScheduleAI is not a traditional app. It is a managed scheduling service with a mobile review and approval interface. The distinction matters: the nurse manager does not build schedules in the app, they review and approve schedules that are built for them.
- For self-serve app users, NurseGrid Manager was built specifically for nurses and has the most relevant default feature set for small hospital nursing departments.
- App cost comparisons should include per-user fees. An app at $5/user/month costs $1,500/year for a 25-nurse department, before accounting for the configuration time required to make healthcare rules work.

## What Makes a Nurse Scheduling App Different from a General Scheduling App?

A general scheduling app solves a coordination problem: who is working when? For most industries, that is the entire problem. For hospital nursing, that is only the first layer.

The second layer is clinical: the right credentials must be on the right shift. An RN who has not completed ACLS recertification cannot staff a monitored bed. A nurse without charge competency sign-off cannot be designated charge. A traveler with a temporary credential holds a different shift-eligibility profile than a permanent staff nurse.

The third layer is compliance: CMS §485.635 requires CAHs to document qualified nursing staff on duty at all times. FLSA Section 7(j) allows healthcare employers to calculate overtime under an 8-and-80 rule rather than the standard 40-hour workweek. Texas Labor Code Chapter 62 adds state-level provisions. None of these are built into a general scheduling app.

The fourth layer is operational: callout handling at a CAH involves checking which available nurses have the right credentials AND are not at risk of overtime before starting the phone tree. A general app with a "broadcast to all available" feature passes all of that calculation back to the nurse manager.

## What Are the 8 Best Nurse Scheduling Apps?

### 1. [SimpleScheduleAI](/)

<img src="/images/blog/best-nurse-scheduling-app/SimpleScheduleAI.png" alt="SimpleScheduleAI scheduling interface" width="1200" height="630" loading="lazy" class="rounded-lg border border-slate-200 my-4" />

SimpleScheduleAI operates as a managed scheduling service with a mobile interface for nurse manager review and approval. The distinction from a traditional app is deliberate: nurses do not build schedules in SimpleScheduleAI. Schedules are built for them and arrive for approval.

**Best for:** CAH nurse managers who want a scheduling solution that handles all four layers (coordination, credentials, compliance, and operational intelligence) without requiring the nurse manager to configure or maintain anything in an app.

**Key advantages:**

- Mobile review and approval interface allows the nurse manager to review proposed schedules, approve them, and handle callout coverage from a phone.
- Callout coverage surface shows a ranked replacement list based on credential match, OT risk (FLSA 8-and-80), and historical availability. No calculation required.
- CMS §485.635 documentation is generated automatically from each published schedule.
- FLSA 8-and-80 overtime enforcement is a default, not a configuration.
- First schedule delivered within 48 hours of staff roster submission.

**Key limitations:**

- Not a self-serve scheduling app. The nurse manager approves and adjusts but does not build schedules directly.
- Pricing not publicly listed. Requires a consultation call.

**Verdict:** The strongest option for a CAH that needs all four scheduling layers handled and wants to minimize time spent in any app on scheduling administration.

**Cost:** Pricing not listed on website. Contact for a quote.

### 2. [NurseGrid Manager](https://nursegrid.com)

<img src="/images/blog/best-nurse-scheduling-app/Nursegrid.png" alt="NurseGrid Manager scheduling app interface" width="1200" height="630" loading="lazy" class="rounded-lg border border-slate-200 my-4" />

NurseGrid Manager is a nurse-specific scheduling platform with one of the strongest mobile apps in the nurse scheduling category. The app is used by nurses to view schedules, pick up open shifts, request time off, and communicate with the scheduling team.

**Best for:** Small hospitals that want a self-serve scheduling system with a genuinely good nurse-facing mobile app and are willing to handle configuration and compliance tracking manually.

**Key advantages:**

- Nurse-focused mobile app design. Nurses find it intuitive compared to general-purpose workforce apps.
- Open shift notification and pickup flow is well-designed for nursing workflows.
- Shift trade and time-off request features work without nurse manager involvement for routine requests.

**Key limitations:**

- Credential enforcement is not automated at the shift level. The nurse manager must verify coverage during schedule review.
- FLSA 8-and-80 overtime rule is not the default. Healthcare employers should verify overtime threshold configuration.
- No CMS documentation generation.

**Verdict:** Best mobile app in the self-serve nurse scheduling category. Strong nurse communication features. Does not cover the compliance and credential enforcement layers.

**Cost:** Contact for pricing.

### 3. [Aladtec](https://www.aladtec.com)

<img src="/images/blog/best-nurse-scheduling-app/Aladtec.png" alt="Aladtec scheduling app interface" width="1200" height="630" loading="lazy" class="rounded-lg border border-slate-200 my-4" />

Aladtec provides web and mobile access to scheduling across all devices. Its mobile app is functional and used by staff in emergency services and healthcare environments for schedule viewing, time-off requests, and shift trades.

**Best for:** Organizations already using Aladtec for multi-department scheduling that want a consistent mobile experience across nursing and other departments.

**Key advantages:**

- Mobile access works reliably for staff schedule viewing and availability management.
- Multi-department scheduling in one system, useful if the CAH wants one tool for nursing, lab, and housekeeping.

**Key limitations:**

- Healthcare-specific configuration (credentials, overtime rules, CMS docs) requires manual backend setup.
- The mobile app is functional but not as polished as NurseGrid or Deputy for day-to-day nurse use.

**Verdict:** Works as a mobile scheduling tool once configured. Configuration burden limits its value for no-IT environments.

**Cost:** Contact for pricing.

### 4. [SmartLinx](https://www.smartlinx.com)

<img src="/images/blog/best-nurse-scheduling-app/Smartlinx.png" alt="SmartLinx scheduling interface" width="1200" height="630" loading="lazy" class="rounded-lg border border-slate-200 my-4" />

SmartLinx provides a full mobile suite alongside its web platform. The app supports schedule viewing, open shift response, time and attendance, and manager approval workflows.

**Best for:** Mid-size hospitals with IT support that want a comprehensive mobile workforce management tool with configurable healthcare rules.

**Key advantages:**

- Full mobile capability for both managers and staff.
- Time and attendance features integrate with the scheduling app, reducing manual payroll reconciliation.
- Configurable credential and coverage enforcement once the system is set up.

**Key limitations:**

- 3-4 month implementation timeline. Not a fast-launch app.
- IT support required for initial configuration and ongoing maintenance.

**Verdict:** Strong mobile platform for hospitals with IT resources. Impractical for CAHs without dedicated technical support.

**Cost:** Contact for pricing.

### 5. [QGenda](https://www.qgenda.com)

<img src="/images/blog/best-nurse-scheduling-app/QGenda.png" alt="QGenda scheduling interface" width="1200" height="630" loading="lazy" class="rounded-lg border border-slate-200 my-4" />

QGenda offers a strong mobile app primarily designed for physician self-scheduling. The nursing module provides similar mobile access for nurse scheduling.

**Best for:** Health systems scheduling physicians and nurses in a single system who need mobile access for both provider groups.

**Key advantages:**

- Well-designed mobile app for provider self-scheduling and on-call management.
- Advanced credential-based rule enforcement once configured.

**Key limitations:**

- Built for health systems, not Critical Access Hospitals. Pricing and implementation reflect enterprise deployment.
- Nursing module is secondary to the physician scheduling core.

**Verdict:** Overbuilt for a CAH nurse scheduling app context.

**Cost:** Contact for pricing.

### 6. [OnShift](https://www.onshift.com) (now part of Workday)

<img src="/images/blog/best-nurse-scheduling-app/Onshift.png" alt="OnShift scheduling app interface" width="1200" height="630" loading="lazy" class="rounded-lg border border-slate-200 my-4" />

OnShift has a strong mobile app in its target market (long-term care) with good shift pickup and open shift notification features. The app's design reflects LTC workflows, not acute care hospital nursing.

**Best for:** Long-term care and assisted living facilities.

**Key advantages:**

- Mobile-first design that LTC staff find easy to adopt.
- Strong open shift broadcast and employee engagement features.

**Key limitations:**

- App design and defaults reflect LTC, not acute care. A CAH would need to work around assumptions that do not match hospital nursing.
- No credential enforcement or CMS CAH documentation.

**Verdict:** Good app for its intended market. Wrong intended market for a CAH.

**Cost:** Contact for pricing.

### 7. [Deputy](https://www.deputy.com)

<img src="/images/blog/best-nurse-scheduling-app/Deputy.png" alt="Deputy scheduling app interface" width="1200" height="630" loading="lazy" class="rounded-lg border border-slate-200 my-4" />

Deputy has one of the best mobile apps in the workforce scheduling category. The nurse-facing interface is clean, shift trades work well, and the onboarding experience is faster than any other tool on this list.

**Best for:** Non-clinical shift environments (retail, hospitality, food service, healthcare reception and housekeeping).

**Key advantages:**

- Excellent mobile app UX. Nurses find it easy to navigate.
- Fastest onboarding of any tool on this list.
- Good shift trade and open shift pickup features.

**Key limitations:**

- No credential tracking. All employees in a role are treated as interchangeable.
- Standard 40-hour workweek overtime threshold. FLSA 8-and-80 is not available.
- No charge nurse designation or CMS documentation.
- Per-user pricing costs $1,500-$2,500/year for a 25-nurse department before accounting for configuration gaps.

**Verdict:** Best app UX, worst fit for hospital nurse scheduling. The quality of the app does not offset the absence of healthcare-specific logic.

**Cost:** From approximately $5/user/month.

### 8. [UKG (formerly Kronos)](https://www.ukg.com)

<img src="/images/blog/best-nurse-scheduling-app/UKG.png" alt="UKG scheduling interface" width="1200" height="630" loading="lazy" class="rounded-lg border border-slate-200 my-4" />

UKG provides a full enterprise mobile workforce management app with scheduling, time and attendance, and compliance reporting. The app is feature-complete but reflects the complexity of the broader UKG platform.

**Best for:** Large hospital systems where the UKG platform is already deployed enterprise-wide.

**Key advantages:**

- Comprehensive mobile access to scheduling, time tracking, and compliance reporting.
- Deep integration with UKG payroll and HR systems.

**Key limitations:**

- Enterprise implementation required. Not applicable for a standalone CAH.
- App complexity reflects a 3,000-employee system, not a 25-nurse department.

**Verdict:** The most capable enterprise scheduling app. The least applicable to a CAH nurse scheduling context.

**Cost:** Enterprise pricing. Contact for quote.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is NurseGrid Manager or Deputy better for a small hospital nursing app?**

NurseGrid Manager is more appropriate for hospital nursing. It was designed for nurses and has credential-aware features that Deputy lacks. Deputy has a better mobile UX but treats all employees in a role as interchangeable, which is not appropriate for hospital nursing where credential differences determine shift eligibility. For a CAH, NurseGrid's nurse-specific design outweighs Deputy's cleaner interface.

**Can a free nurse scheduling app work for a Critical Access Hospital?**

Free scheduling apps (like NurseGrid's staff-facing app or basic tiers of general tools) cover schedule viewing and shift swap communication. They do not cover credential enforcement, FLSA 8-and-80 overtime, or CMS documentation. For a CAH, using a free or low-cost app without those features means those tasks are being handled manually elsewhere, usually by the nurse manager in a spreadsheet. The app cost is low. The labor cost of compensating for what the app cannot do is not.

**How does a managed service differ from an app for daily nurse manager use?**

A scheduling app requires the nurse manager to log in, build or edit schedules, review requests, and check compliance. A managed service delivers a completed schedule for review. The nurse manager's daily use shifts from construction to approval. With SimpleScheduleAI, the typical daily interaction is a 5-10 minute review of any callout ranking updates or schedule adjustments the service team has flagged for approval. The nurse manager is not in an app building schedules. They are reviewing and approving work the service team produced.

**Does OnShift's mobile app work for hospital nursing if we're switching from LTC?**

OnShift's app was designed for LTC workflows. The shift patterns, credential categories, and coverage assumptions in the app reflect skilled nursing and assisted living environments. For an acute care CAH, those defaults would need to be reconfigured to reflect hospital nursing patterns (12-hour shifts, ACLS/charge requirements, acute care staffing ratios). The mobile UX quality is reasonable, but the underlying model is mismatched to acute care.

**What happens to scheduling app access if we transition from SimpleScheduleAI to a different system?**

SimpleScheduleAI provides data export in standard formats at any time. Staff roster data, scheduling history, and credential records are available for export if the hospital transitions to a different system. The process is similar to transitioning from any scheduling platform: you export your data and import it into the new system. SimpleScheduleAI does not use proprietary data formats that would create a lock-in barrier.
