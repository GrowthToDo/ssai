A critical access hospital (CAH) is a CMS-designated rural hospital with 25 or fewer acute care beds, located at least 35 miles from the nearest hospital (or 15 miles by secondary road in mountainous terrain), and providing 24-hour emergency care every day of the year. CAHs receive cost-based Medicare reimbursement rather than the prospective payment rates that apply to standard acute care hospitals, a material financial advantage. As of 2024, approximately 1,350 CAHs operate in the United States, with 84 located in Texas. The designation is governed by [42 CFR Part 485, Subpart F](https://www.cms.gov/medicare/health-safety-standards/certification-compliance/critical-access-hospitals).

## How Critical Access Hospitals Work?

CMS established the CAH designation through the Balanced Budget Act of 1997 in response to a wave of rural hospital closures in the 1980s and early 1990s. To qualify, a hospital must meet all of the following criteria:

- **Location:** Rural area, at least 35 miles from the nearest hospital (or 15 miles in mountainous terrain or where only secondary roads exist)
- **Bed limit:** No more than 25 acute care inpatient beds (swing beds for skilled nursing are counted separately)
- **Length of stay:** Average annual inpatient stay of 96 hours or less
- **Emergency care:** 24-hour emergency services, 365 days per year
- **State designation:** Must be designated by the state as a necessary provider

The financial benefit of CAH status is substantial. While standard hospitals are reimbursed at fixed prospective payment rates (often below actual cost), CAHs receive 101% of their reasonable Medicare costs. This cost-based reimbursement model is the primary reason rural hospitals actively pursue and protect their CAH designation.

Staffing requirements are codified at [42 CFR §485.635](https://www.cms.gov/medicare/health-safety-standards/certification-compliance/critical-access-hospitals). CAHs must have a registered nurse on duty or immediately available on call at all times. Compliance with this requirement must be documented and surveyable, which has direct implications for how nurse scheduling is structured and recorded.

The [HRSA Flex Program](https://data.hrsa.gov/topics/health-workforce/shortage-areas) provides technical assistance and grant funding to CAHs in most states. In Texas, [TORCH (Texas Organization of Rural and Community Hospitals)](https://www.torchnet.org) supports CAHs with compliance guidance, workforce resources, and advocacy.

## How CAHs Differ from Community Hospitals?

The differences between CAHs and standard community hospitals extend well beyond bed count.

**Reimbursement model:** Community hospitals receive Medicare Prospective Payment System (PPS) rates, which are fixed regardless of actual costs. CAHs receive cost-based reimbursement at 101% of reasonable costs. This means every dollar of unnecessary overtime or inefficient staffing hits a CAH's margin more directly, but it also means cost management is rewarded rather than penalized.

**Bed limit:** CAHs are capped at 25 acute care beds by federal regulation. Standard community hospitals have no such federal cap; state certificate-of-need laws may apply separately.

**Location requirement:** CAHs must be rural and geographically isolated. Community hospitals can be urban, suburban, or rural without restriction.

**Staffing requirements:** The §485.635 requirement for continuous RN availability is CAH-specific. Standard hospitals are governed by CMS Conditions of Participation at 42 CFR Part 482, which have different nurse staffing language. Texas also applies its own nursing facility regulations, but CAH-specific federal requirements add a compliance layer that community hospitals do not face.

**Administrative capacity:** Most CAHs operate with lean administrative staff. The nurse manager at a 20-bed CAH often handles scheduling, compliance documentation, and direct patient care simultaneously, a workload that would be distributed across multiple roles at a larger facility.

## Why CAH Status Matters for Nurse Managers?

For nurse managers at critical access hospitals, the CAH designation is not an abstract regulatory category, it shapes daily operations.

The §485.635 requirement that a registered nurse be on duty or on call at all times means that gaps in coverage are a compliance violation, not just an inconvenience. Every shift needs to be covered by someone with the right credentials, and that coverage needs to be documented. During a CMS survey, the scheduling record is evidence.

Cost-based reimbursement means the hospital's financial health is tied directly to its cost structure. Unnecessary overtime is money the hospital pays that shows up directly in its Medicare cost reports. A nurse manager who reduces overtime by 15% is not just saving budget, they are improving the hospital's reimbursement sustainability.

The small team size at most CAHs means there is little margin for error. With 10-20 nurses on staff, a single unplanned absence can require a full schedule rebuild. Per diem pools, callout protocols, and documented on-call availability are operational necessities, not optional enhancements.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What qualifies a hospital as a critical access hospital?**
A hospital must be located in a rural area at least 35 miles from the nearest hospital, maintain 25 or fewer acute care beds, provide 24/7 emergency care, keep average inpatient stays under 96 hours, and receive state designation as a necessary provider. CMS administers the federal designation under 42 CFR Part 485, Subpart F.

**Q: How many critical access hospitals are in Texas?**
As of 2024, Texas has 84 critical access hospitals, the second-highest count of any state. They are concentrated in West Texas, the Panhandle, and rural East Texas. TORCH (Texas Organization of Rural and Community Hospitals) maintains a current list and serves as the state's CAH support network.

**Q: What are the staffing requirements for critical access hospitals?**
Under 42 CFR §485.635, CAHs must have a registered nurse on duty or immediately on call at all times. The CAH must document compliance with this requirement. State nursing regulations layer on top of federal requirements. Texas-specific rules from HHSC apply alongside CMS conditions of participation.
