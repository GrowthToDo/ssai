## The $26,000 No One Budgets For: Scheduling at a Critical Access Hospital

It is 5 a.m. A nurse calls in sick. At a 25-bed hospital there is no staffing office to absorb it, so the nurse manager does what she always does. She pulls up the list, starts calling down it, and tries to fill a 7 a.m. shift before it becomes a coverage gap. Then she finishes the calls, documents the change, and goes and works her own shift on the floor.

That hour does not show up anywhere. Not on an invoice. Not as a line item. Not in any budget meeting. And it is one hour of a number most critical access hospitals never put on paper.

Here is the number. A nurse manager at a small hospital typically spends 8 to 12 hours a week on scheduling: building the rotation, working the callout phone tree, handling swap requests, and pulling the documentation a CMS survey will ask for. At a loaded leadership rate of around $50 an hour, that is roughly $26,000 a year. Not in software. Not in agency fees. In one person's time, spent on administrative work instead of patient care and staff retention.

I call it the shadow salary, because that is what it is. The hospital is already paying it. It is just buried inside a clinical leader's week where no one counts it.

Why does it stay invisible?

Three reasons.

First, there is no invoice. Anything a hospital writes a check for gets scrutinized. Anything that is already inside a salary you are paying does not. The cost is real, but it never crosses a desk where someone asks "is this the best use of this money."

Second, the visible part gets blamed on the wrong thing. Overtime from phone-tree coverage does show up on the P&L. But it gets logged as a scheduling problem or a staffing problem, not as a symptom of a manual coverage process that depends on one stretched person making calls at 5 a.m. You cannot fix a process you have miscategorized as a number.

Third, the person paying it cannot raise her hand. The nurse manager absorbing those 8 to 12 hours is the same person who would have to make the case to change it. She is on the floor. She does not have a free afternoon to build a business case for getting her own afternoons back.

None of this is a software complaint. It is an operations observation. A 25-bed hospital is not a small version of a 250-bed hospital. It has no float pool, no IT department, no dedicated scheduler, and a nurse manager who is also a clinician. The work still has to happen, so it lands on the one person who cannot say no to it. That is the structural reason the cost hides.

The first step is not buying anything. It is counting. For one week, have your nurse manager log the time: schedule building, callout coverage, swap requests, pre-survey documentation. Total it. Multiply by a loaded hourly rate. That single number changes the conversation, because now it is on paper instead of inside a salary. I walked through the full breakdown, including the phone-tree math and where the overtime creep comes from, here: https://simplescheduleai.com/blog/healthcare-scheduling-crisis

Once you have the number, you can decide what it is worth to get it back. But you cannot decide anything while it stays invisible.
