# SimpleScheduleAI — Backlinks Playbook

Last updated: 2026-05-16. Owner: Pradeep. Workstream F of the SEO plan.

This is a do-this-now document. Every section tells you the exact target, the exact action, and gives you the exact text to send or publish. Solo-founder realistic: budget ~3 to 5 hours/week. Goal: **5 relevant healthcare-adjacent links in 30 days**, then a steady 3 to 5/month.

---

## 1. Reality and strategy (read once)

- The domain authority is near zero. You cannot win on volume or compete for hard links yet. You win on **relevance and specificity**: links from rural-health, CAH, nursing-ops, and Texas-hospital contexts are worth far more than generic SaaS directories.
- One genuinely relevant link from a rural health association beats fifty directory links. Do not buy links. Do not use private blog networks. Both are detectable and will cap the site permanently.
- Do **not** mass-disavow the existing spam backlinks. Google discounts low-quality links passively. Only disavow if a manual action appears in Search Console. Touching the disavow file without a manual action usually does more harm than good.
- Brand mentions matter even without a link. An unlinked "SimpleScheduleAI" mention on a healthcare-ops site still feeds entity recognition (this is a GEO/AI-search signal). Pursue mentions, not just hyperlinks.
- Every link or mention must point to a real, useful page. Best landing targets, in order: `/` (home), `/critical-access-hospital-scheduling` (Hub 2, the niche moat), `/nurse-scheduling-software` (Hub 1), `/ai-nurse-scheduling` (Hub 3), `/blog/healthcare-scheduling-crisis`, `/about/pradeep-pandey` (for author/E-E-A-T links).

## 3. Tier 1 targets — highest relevance, do these first

These are the links that actually move a CAH-niche site. Work top to bottom.

### 3.1 TORCH — Texas Organization of Rural & Community Hospitals

- URL: https://www.torchnet.org — look for "Associate Members", "Vendor/Partner", or "Resources".
- Why: the single most on-target organization in existence for this product. Texas + rural + community hospitals.
- Action: email their membership/partnership contact (use the **Association / vendor directory** template in §8a). Ask about associate/vendor membership and any online vendor directory or resource listing. Associate membership may have a fee — evaluate; even the listing alone is worth it.
- Submit: the §2 data block + the one-liner.
- Expected: medium effort, possibly paid, very high relevance. Pursue first.

### 3.2 National Rural Health Association (NRHA)

- URL: https://www.ruralhealth.us — "Membership" → corporate/partner, and any "Rural Health Resources" pages.
- Why: national rural-health authority; a corporate member listing or resource mention is highly topical.
- Action: §8a template. Ask about corporate membership and resource-directory inclusion.
- Expected: medium effort, likely paid membership, high relevance.

### 3.3 HRSA Rural Health resources / Rural Health Information Hub

- URLs: https://www.ruralhealthinfo.org (RHIhub — runs resource and "Rural Health Models & Innovations" libraries) and HRSA rural workforce pages.
- Why: `.gov`-adjacent, extremely authoritative and topical. RHIhub accepts relevant tools/resources for its libraries.
- Action: find RHIhub "Contact" / "Suggest a resource". Pitch SimpleScheduleAI as a workforce/scheduling resource for Critical Access Hospitals. Use §8b (resource-page) template, adapted.
- Expected: lower acceptance rate, but a single inclusion here is a flagship link. Worth repeated polite follow-up.

### 3.4 Flex Monitoring Team

- URL: https://www.flexmonitoring.org — CAH-specific research and resources.
- Why: CAH is literally their entire focus.
- Action: there may be no vendor listing; instead pursue a **mention** — offer Pradeep as a source/commenter on CAH staffing, or contribute a data point. §8d (expert source) template.
- Expected: relationship play, not a quick form. Long-term high value.

### 3.5 Texas Hospital Association (THA)

- URL: https://www.tha.org — "Endorsed/Partner vendors", "Marketplace", or member resources.
- Why: Texas + hospital. THA often has an endorsed-vendor or marketplace program.
- Action: §8a template to their partnerships/marketplace contact.
- Expected: may require vetting or fee; high relevance for the Texas angle.

## 4. Tier 2 targets — content and PR driven

### 4.1 HARO / Qwoted / Featured (journalist source requests)

- URLs: https://www.featured.com (formerly HARO-style), https://www.qwoted.com.
- Why: respond to journalist queries about healthcare staffing, nurse burnout, rural hospitals, AI in healthcare. A used quote = a contextual link or a strong branded mention on a real publication.
- Action: Pradeep creates a source profile (use §2 bio). Check queries 3x/week. Respond only to genuinely relevant queries with a specific, data-backed, non-promotional answer. Template in §8e.
- Cadence: 15 minutes, 3x/week. This is the highest ROI ongoing activity for a solo founder.

### 4.2 Podcast guesting

- Targets: rural health podcasts, healthcare-ops/nurse-leadership podcasts, health-IT podcasts, small-business/founder podcasts in healthcare.
- Why: show notes links + audience + repurposable content + authority.
- Action: build a list of 10 shows; pitch with §8c. Lead with a specific guest angle (see §5 themes), not "I have a product."
- Cadence: 5 pitches/week for 2 weeks, then follow up.

### 4.3 Niche resource-page link building

- Find pages that list "nurse scheduling tools", "rural hospital resources", "tools for nurse managers", "CAH operations resources".
- Search operators (run in Google, paste into a list):
  - `"nurse scheduling" (resources OR tools) inurl:resources`
  - `"critical access hospital" (resources OR vendors OR tools)`
  - `"rural hospital" "resources" -inurl:(signup login)`
  - `"nurse manager" tools "scheduling" intitle:resources`
- Action: for each genuinely relevant page, send §8b. Only pitch where the tool genuinely belongs on that page.

## 5. The guest post — full specification

One well-placed guest article on a healthcare-ops publication is the single biggest Tier-2 win. Do exactly one, do it well.

### 5.1 Where to pitch (in priority order)

1. **Becker's Hospital Review** (beckershospitalreview.com) — contributed content; large healthcare-exec audience.
2. **HealthLeaders** (healthleadersmedia.com) — nursing/workforce focus.
3. **Fierce Healthcare** (fiercehealthcare.com) — health-tech and operations.
4. **HIT Consultant** (hitconsultant.net) — health-IT, accepts contributed thought leadership.
5. **RevCycleIntelligence / TechTarget healthcare** — operations and cost angle.
6. Rural-health blogs and state hospital association newsletters (lower bar, still relevant) — good fallbacks if 1 to 5 do not respond.

### 5.2 The angle (do not pitch a product, pitch a problem)

**Working title options:**

- "What Enterprise Scheduling Tools Get Wrong About 25-Bed Hospitals"
- "The Hidden $26,000 Scheduling Cost Inside Every Critical Access Hospital"
- "Why Rural Hospitals Keep Buying Scheduling Software They Cannot Run"

Lead angle: enterprise workforce platforms are sold into rural/critical-access hospitals through health-system contracts, but a 25-bed hospital with no IT department and a clinically-active nurse manager cannot operate them, so they fall back to spreadsheets. This is an operations and cost problem, not a software-feature problem.

### 5.3 Full outline (write to this; ~1,200 to 1,500 words for a contributed piece)

1. **Hook (120 words):** A nurse manager at a 25-bed hospital working a callout phone tree at 5 AM. Concrete, real, no product.
2. **The structural problem (250 words):** CAH constraints — no float pool, no IT, no HR, clinically-active manager, CMS §485.635 RN-on-call requirement, Texas overtime rules. Cite CMS and HRSA rural workforce data (link to primary sources, not to SimpleScheduleAI).
3. **Why enterprise tools fail here (300 words):** Built for 200+ bed systems with dedicated scheduling staff and 6 to 12 month implementations. Extended to rural affiliates via system contracts. The mismatch is operational, not feature depth. Honest: enterprise tools are correct for enterprises.
4. **The real cost (250 words):** The 8 to 12 weekly hours of nurse-manager time, the ~$26,000/year shadow salary, overtime creep from phone-tree coverage. Frame as a leadership-time and patient-care problem.
5. **What actually fits a 25-bed hospital (300 words):** Criteria, not a pitch — week-one usability, no IT dependency, state compliance built in, fast callout coverage, human approval retained. Mention the managed-service model as a category (one sentence naming SimpleScheduleAI as an example of that model, no more).
6. **Close (150 words):** A checklist a CAH administrator can act on this quarter.
7. **Author bio (the link lives here):** Use §2 founder bio. One link to `https://simplescheduleai.com` or `/about/pradeep-pandey`. One link only. Editorial guest posts that stuff links get rejected or nofollowed.

### 5.4 Rules

- 90% education, 10% (or less) product. One link in the bio, optionally one contextual link to a non-commercial resource page (`/critical-access-hospital-scheduling` is acceptable as it reads as a guide).
- Every statistic must link to a primary source (CMS, HRSA, Texas DSHS, ANA). No unsourced claims — editors check.
- Match the §2 bio exactly so the author entity stays consistent across the web.

## 6. LinkedIn — Pradeep's owned channel (do this weekly, it compounds)

LinkedIn articles and consistent posting build the founder entity, earn profile-driven mentions, and are the most controllable channel. Publish under Pradeep's personal profile, not the company page (personal reach is far higher).

### 6.1 Three cornerstone articles (publish one per week, weeks 1 to 3)

**Article 1 — "The $26,000 No One Budgets For: Scheduling at a Critical Access Hospital"**

- Hook: the shadow salary buried in a nurse manager's week.
- Body: break down the 8 to 12 hours; phone-tree math; overtime creep; why it is invisible on the P&L.
- Link: to `/blog/healthcare-scheduling-crisis`.
- CTA: "If you run a 25-bed hospital, reply with how many hours your manager loses to this."

**Article 2 — "Can Nurses Trust an AI-Built Schedule? An Honest Answer"**

- Hook: the most common staff objection when AI scheduling is proposed.
- Body: what AI computes, what it cannot judge, why human approval stays. Counter-intuitive honesty (this earns shares and AI citations).
- Link: to `/ai-nurse-scheduling` and `/blog/can-nurses-trust-ai-generated-schedule`.
- CTA: invite nurse managers to share their fairness concerns.

**Article 3 — "Why Rural Hospitals Keep Buying Software They Cannot Run"**

- Hook: the enterprise-contract trap (UKG/enterprise extended to CAH affiliates).
- Body: the operate-it-yourself vs delivered-to-you distinction; managed service as a category.
- Link: to `/critical-access-hospital-scheduling`.
- CTA: ask which enterprise tool they got stuck with.

### 6.2 Posting cadence (ongoing, ~30 min/week)

- 2 short posts/week: one operational insight (no link), one that links to a blog post or article.
- Comment substantively on 3 posts/week from rural-health, CHA/THA, nurse-leadership voices. Relationships precede links.
- Tag relevant organizations only when genuinely warranted. Never spam-tag.

## 7. Reddit and communities (slow, authentic, no link-dropping)

- Subreddits: r/nursing, r/nursemanagers (if active), r/healthIT, r/hospitaladministration, r/ruralhealth (if active).
- Rule: never drop a link cold. Build comment history answering scheduling/staffing questions with genuinely useful, specific answers. A link is acceptable only when someone explicitly asks for a tool and it is the honest best answer for their stated case, and even then disclose the affiliation ("I built X, biased, but here is the honest tradeoff").
- One genuinely helpful, disclosed answer is worth more than ten removed self-promo posts. Moderators ban fast; the cost of getting this wrong is permanent in that community.
- Cadence: 15 minutes, 2x/week, answer-first.

## 12. Do / Don't

**Do**

- Personalize the first line of every outreach email to the specific recipient/page.
- Lead with usefulness (a free resource, a data point, an honest answer) before any ask.
- Keep the §2 entity data byte-identical everywhere.
- Pursue mentions, not only links — unlinked brand mentions still build the entity.
- Track everything; follow up at most twice.

**Don't**

- Don't buy links, use PBNs, or do reciprocal-link schemes.
- Don't mass-disavow existing spam (no manual action = leave it alone).
- Don't link-drop in Reddit/communities or stuff links in guest posts.
- Don't fake a Google Business Profile address.
- Don't multi-submit the same guest post to competing publications simultaneously.
- Don't send a third follow-up.
